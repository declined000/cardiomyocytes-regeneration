2. METHODS
================================================================================

2.1 Staining Analysis
----------------------------------------
  Phalloidin: classical pipeline -- DAPI thresholding + watershed
  nuclei segmentation, nuclei-seeded watershed for cell boundaries.
  16 per-cell metrics (morphology, F-actin intensity, texture).
  Multinucleation: Cellpose v3 (cyto2 model, GPU) + DAPI nuclei.
  Alpha-actinin: INCONCLUSIVE (no usable signal in any image).

2.2 Brightfield Video Analysis
----------------------------------------
  Farneback optical flow (OpenCV) on 2x-downsampled BF frames.
  Convergence map (neg. divergence) for active contraction centers.
  Per-center foci detection via thresholded convergence + CC labeling.
  Beat detection: scipy.signal.find_peaks, 1.5s settling exclusion.
  Classification (expert-validated): active_beating,
    individual_cells_beating, non_contractile, no_beating.
  Methods aligned with Huebsch 2015, Czirok 2017, Sala 2018.

2.3 Fluorescence Calcium Imaging Analysis
----------------------------------------
  GCaMP6f intensity-based pipeline (NOT optical flow).
  Background subtraction: 5-pixel border strip (all four edges) for non-cell baseline.
  Photobleach correction: mono-exponential fit y=a*exp(-t/tau)+c.
  F0 baseline: rolling 5th percentile (2s window).
  dF/F0 = (F - F0) / F0 for each timepoint.
  Peak detection: scipy.signal.find_peaks with adaptive prominence.
  Per-transient kinetics: dF/F0 amplitude, Time to Peak (TTP),
    CaTD50, CaTD90, decay time constant (tau), upstroke/decay velocity.
  Aggregate metrics: beat rate (BPM), inter-beat interval CV (IBI CV).
  Methods aligned with Psaras/CalTrack 2021, Bedut 2022.

  Pipeline: background subtraction -> mono-exponential photobleach
  correction -> rolling 5th-percentile F0 -> dF/F0 -> peak detection

  KEY SCIENTIFIC QUESTION:
  Do GCaMP6f cells show calcium transients (FL) despite no
  visible synchronized contraction (BF)? This distinguishes between:
    a) No calcium cycling -- cells are truly non-functional
    b) Calcium present but mechanically uncoupled -- cells

  the CaV1 perturbation hypothesis (Yang et al. Nat Commun 2018),

  LOW-FPS IMPACT ASSESSMENT:
  4 of 8 FL videos have FPS < 5 (range 1.1-3.2 FPS).
  At 2-3 FPS, calcium transients (~300-500 ms fast phases)
  are sampled by only 1-2 frames per event. This means:
    - TRANSIENT DETECTION: still possible (amplitude changes are
      visible even at low temporal resolution)
    - KINETIC PARAMETERS (TTP, CaTD50, CaTD90): UNRELIABLE at
      low FPS -- temporal precision is limited to 1/FPS
      (e.g., 500 ms at 2 FPS vs 80 ms at 12.5 FPS)
    - TRANSIENT COUNT: may overcount at low FPS due to aliasing
      (e.g., C3-day6-gold-fl detected 18 'transients' at 3.2 FPS)
    - BEAT RATE: reliable (long recording compensates for
      poor per-beat resolution)

  USABLE-FPS-ONLY ANALYSIS (>= 5 FPS, n=4):

  INTERPRETATION: WHAT USABLE-ONLY NUMBERS CHANGE
  Excluding low-FPS videos does NOT weaken any conclusion;
  it STRENGTHENS them:

    1. EMD finding: 7/8 -> 4/4 (100%). The one 'no transients'
       video was at 1.1 FPS -- likely too slow to detect, not
       a true negative. Core conclusion unchanged.

    2. Amplitude: usable mean dF/F0 = 0.0981 vs all-video mean = 0.0833.
       Usable videos show HIGHER amplitude (+18%), because low-FPS
       videos under-sample transient peaks, compressing apparent
       amplitude. True calcium signals are stronger than reported
       when all videos are pooled.

    3. Temporal progression REVERSES apparent trend:
       All videos: day2=0.110 > day6=0.091 > day8=0.045 (declining)
       Usable only: day2=0.086 < day6=0.216 > day8=0.045 (peak at day6)
       The all-video trend was an ARTIFACT: day6 had the strongest
       usable signal (0.216 dF/F0) but its average was diluted by
       two low-FPS recordings (0.023 and 0.033). The usable-only
       data shows calcium activity PEAKING at day 6, consistent
       with maturation-driven calcium handling improvement
       (Hwang et al. 2015), before declining at day 8.

    4. Gold substrate effect is STRONGER with usable data:
       Gold usable: dF/F0=0.117 vs Gold all: 0.102 (+15%)
       Non-poled usable: dF/F0=0.041 vs all: 0.037 (+10%)
       Gold/non-poled ratio: usable=2.9x vs all=2.8x
       The substrate effect holds regardless; gold-coated PVDF
       consistently produces ~3x higher calcium amplitudes.

    5. Beat regularity IMPROVES in usable data:
       IBI CV: usable=30.9% vs all=43.0%
       Lower CV = more regular beating, consistent with
       properly resolved transients rather than aliased noise.

  not create false positives. All primary findings (EMD, substrate

  CRITICAL FINDING: ELECTROMECHANICAL DISSOCIATION
  BF analysis: 0/7 GCaMP6f videos showed active synchronized beating
    (1/7 showed individual cells beating — C3-day8-non-Jaz.avi)
  FL analysis: 7/8 GCaMP6f videos show calcium transients
    [usable FPS only: 4/4]
  CONCLUSION: GCaMP6f cells HAVE calcium cycling but are
  MECHANICALLY UNCOUPLED -- calcium transients are present
  without visible synchronized sarcomeric contraction. This is consistent
  with 'electromechanical dissociation' (EMD), a phenomenon
  reported in immature hiPSC-CMs where electrical/calcium
  signaling precedes mechanical coupling (Lee et al. 2019,
  Ronaldson-Bouchard et al. 2018).

  This result REFINES the CaV1 perturbation hypothesis:
  since calcium transients ARE present, GCaMP6f does not
  fully abolish CaV1-mediated calcium entry. Instead, the
  perturbation may be PARTIAL: sufficient Ca2+ enters to
  generate detectable fluorescence transients via GCaMP6f,
  but the amplitude/kinetics are insufficient to trigger
  full excitation-contraction coupling (ECC). Alternative
  explanations include:
    - Immature sarcomere organization in GCaMP6f cells
    - Reduced myofibrillar density (F-actin/sarcomeric actin)
    - Impaired calcium-induced calcium release (CICR) from SR
    - Disrupted T-tubule development (common in hiPSC-CMs)

          day2=0.086 -> day6=0.216 -> day8=0.045
        Within the usable-FPS subset, calcium activity peaks at
        day 6 rather than day 2.
        The all-video day6 average (0.091) was diluted by two
        low-FPS recordings (0.023 and 0.033) that under-sampled
        transient peaks.

        This usable-FPS pattern is consistent with
        maturation-associated calcium handling improvement
        (Hwang et al. 2015): calcium cycling develops during
        days 2-6, peaks as SR calcium stores mature, then
        declines by day 8 -- potentially due to GCaMP6f
        photobleaching, substrate degradation, or cumulative
        CaV1 perturbation from chronic GCaMP6f expression.

     5. COMPARISON WITH XR1 BF CONTRACTILITY:
        XR1 (BF): 11/16 active beating, increasing amplitude
        day2 -> day8 (functional maturation)
        GCaMP6f (BF): 0/7 active beating (1/7 individual cells beating)
        GCaMP6f (FL): 7/8 show Ca2+ transients
        Together, these modalities show that:
          - XR1 clearly shows contraction in BF, but calcium cycling
            was not measured in XR1 here
          - GCaMP6f shows calcium cycling WITHOUT synchronized
            contraction under the present readouts
        The uncoupling is likely multifactorial:
          - CaV1 perturbation by GCaMP6f CaM moiety (Yang 2018)
          - Potentially lower sarcomeric maturity
          - Immature SR calcium release (CICR) machinery
          - Absent/immature T-tubule network

4. DISCUSSION
================================================================================

4.1 SUBSTRATE-DEPENDENT ADHESION AND FUNCTION
----------------------------------------
  Both modalities point in the same direction: gold-coated PVDF
  has worse cell adhesion than non-poled PVDF or bare plastic.
  Staining shows rounder cells (higher circularity) on gold,
  while video shows more floating cells (flow/drift) and fewer
  actively beating wells. Non-poled PVDF supported the strongest
  contractions observed in this dataset (median 3.8 px amplitude).
  Gold surfaces typically require protein coating (fibronectin,
  laminin) for cardiomyocyte attachment (Tian et al. 2022).

4.2 DEVICE AND PIEZOELECTRIC EFFECTS
----------------------------------------
  Staining: Au-PVDF Poled Pulsed (device) shows significantly
  lower actin coherency vs all other conditions (p<0.001),
  consistent with altered cytoskeletal organization. Because
  alpha-actinin was inconclusive, a specific sarcomeric
  interpretation remains tentative.

  Video: on-device median amplitude (3.60 px) vs control (2.16 px).
  Note: the aggregate device-vs-control comparison pools
  wells with different substrates, so paired within-well
  comparisons (Fig V5) provide the cleanest assessment.

  PIEZOELECTRIC-RELEVANT COMPARISON (B2, gold-poled PVDF):
  The B2 within-well pair (pulsed vs unpulsed, same gold-poled
  substrate, same cell line) is the cleanest piezoelectric
  comparison in this dataset. Pulsed amplitude is 2.7x higher
  (2.96 vs 1.08 px). Because the substrate is identical, this
  amplification is attributable to pulsation acting on the
  poled piezoelectric film. Fully isolating the piezo
  contribution from mechanical pulsation alone would require
  a nonpoled-unpulsed control on the same substrate, which
  was not included in this run (addressed in Experiment 2).

4.3 CELL LINE VARIABILITY: XR1 vs GCaMP6f
----------------------------------------
  XR1 CMs beat spontaneously from day 2 with increasing
  amplitude through day 8 (functional maturation).
  GCaMP6f CMs showed NO synchronized beating at any timepoint (BF);
  1/7 videos showed individual cell beating.

  MECHANISTIC HYPOTHESIS:
  The GCaMP6f line carries a genetically encoded calcium indicator
  (GECI) based on calmodulin (CaM) fused to cpEGFP. Yang et al.
  (Nat Commun 2018, doi:10.1038/s41467-018-03719-6) demonstrated
  that GCaMP -- including GCaMP6f specifically -- interferes with
  L-type calcium channel (CaV1) gating through its CaM moiety:

    1. GCaMP acts as an IMPAIRED apoCaM and Ca2+/CaM, both
       critical for CaV1 function
    2. This perturbs calcium-dependent inactivation (CDI) and
       voltage-gated activation (VGA) of L-type Ca2+ channels
    3. Chronic expression causes Ca2+ dysregulation, aberrant
       nuclear accumulation, and disrupted E-T coupling
    4. GCaMP2 transgenic mice exhibited cardiomegaly/hypertrophy
       (Tallini et al. 2006, cited in Yang et al. 2018)

  FLUORESCENCE DATA CONSTRAINS THIS HYPOTHESIS:
  FL analysis reveals 7/8 GCaMP6f videos show
  calcium transients, indicating that GCaMP6f cells are not
  calcium-silent at the field level. This produces a nuanced picture:

    - MEASURABLE Ca2+ TRANSIENTS are PRESENT (FL detected)
    - SYNCHRONIZED CONTRACTION is ABSENT (0/7 BF videos show active beating;
      1/7 showed individual cells beating — C3-day8-non-Jaz.avi)
    - Therefore: APPARENT ELECTROMECHANICAL DISSOCIATION under
      these readouts at the tissue level

  This pattern is consistent with partial CaV1 perturbation,
  immature excitation-contraction coupling, or both. The
  fluorescence data alone do not identify the exact source of
  Ca2+ entry. One possible explanation is reduced L-type trigger
  current and lower CICR gain, but this was not measured here.

  ROBUSTNESS CHECK: When restricting to usable-FPS videos
  (>= 5 FPS, n=4), all 4/4 show calcium transients (vs 7/8
  with all videos). The one 'no transients' video was at
  1.1 FPS -- too slow to capture fast calcium events. Usable
  videos show higher amplitude (mean dF/F0=0.098 vs 0.083),
  more regular beating (IBI CV=31% vs 43%), and reveal that
  calcium activity peaks at day 6 (dF/F0=0.216) rather than
  day 2 -- a pattern that was masked when low-FPS videos
  diluted the aggregate. This strengthens the EMD conclusion:
  the usable-FPS subset suggests stronger and more organized
  calcium activity in GCaMP6f cells than the all-video
  aggregate suggests, yet still insufficient for synchronized
  mechanical contraction.

  This is further supported by Lee et al. (Nat Commun 2019)
  and Ronaldson-Bouchard et al. (Nature 2018) who show that
  calcium transients develop BEFORE mechanical coupling in
  hiPSC-CM maturation, consistent with the possibility that
  GCaMP6f cells occupy a pre-coupling-like state under these
  readouts.

  Future experiments should consider GCaMP-X (Yang et al. 2018)
  or chemical dyes (Fluo-4, Fura-2) to avoid CaV1 perturbation.

4.4 NON-CONTRACTILE MOTION
----------------------------------------
  6 videos showed non-contractile motion: periodic passive
  displacement without sarcomeric contraction, primarily in loosely
  attached cells. Convergence analysis (Czirok et al. 2017) assumes
  substrate-adhered cells; floating cells produce false-positive
  signals. Manual expert validation was essential.

4.5 STRUCTURE-FUNCTION-CALCIUM RELATIONSHIP
----------------------------------------
  Cross-referencing all three modalities reveals a hierarchy of
  functional maturation in hiPSC-derived cardiomyocytes:

  MATURATION HIERARCHY (observed in this experiment):
    Level 1: F-actin organization (staining) -- PRESENT in all conditions
    Level 2: Calcium cycling (FL) -- PRESENT in GCaMP6f cells
    Level 3: Synchronized contraction (BF) -- ABSENT in GCaMP6f
             (1/7 showed individual cell beating), PRESENT in XR1

  This hierarchy mirrors the known developmental sequence in
  cardiomyocyte maturation (Karbassi et al. Nat Rev Cardiol 2020):
  cytoskeletal assembly -> ion channel expression -> calcium
  handling -> sarcomeric organization -> functional ECC.

  The strongest BF beating (non-poled PVDF) was not the condition
  with the highest F-actin intensity or lowest coherency,
  confirming that functional maturation involves more than
  cytoskeletal organization alone. The FL data adds a crucial
  intermediate readout showing that calcium handling and
  contraction can be dissociated, especially in cell lines
  with genetic perturbations (GCaMP6f).

  Sheehy et al. (2020) showed that myofibrillar structural
  variability can strongly influence contractile function in
  hiPSC-CMs, independent of calcium dynamics. In our dataset,
  XR1 staining is compatible with that framework, but GCaMP6f
  structure was not imaged, so any myofibrillar explanation for
  the GCaMP6f phenotype remains hypothetical.

4.6 METHODOLOGICAL NOTES
----------------------------------------
  BF video:
  - Farneback optical flow: established for BF CM video (Maddah 2015)
  - Convergence: discriminates active vs passive (Czirok 2017)
  - Per-center foci: extends to sparse cultures (Huebsch 2015)
  - MUSCLEMOTION: our metrics align (Sala 2018)

  FL calcium imaging:
  - Intensity-based pipeline (NOT optical flow) -- appropriate for
    GCaMP6f where fluorescence directly reports [Ca2+]i
  - Background subtraction: 5-pixel border strip (all four edges)
  - Photobleach correction: mono-exponential fit (standard for GECIs)
  - F0: rolling 5th percentile (2s window) -- Psaras/CalTrack (2021)
  - Peak detection: scipy.signal.find_peaks with adaptive thresholding
  - Kinetics: TTP, CaTD50, CaTD90, decay tau -- Bedut et al. (2022)
  - Low-FPS videos (< 5 FPS) were processed but flagged; kinetic
    parameters from these should be interpreted cautiously

  Staining:
  - Classical segmentation validated by visual QC

5. LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

  General:
  1.  N=1 biological replicate per condition (single experiment)
  2.  Unbalanced design (no 'Nonpoled Un-pulsed' control)
  3.  Old cells from external lab (not freshly differentiated)
  4.  B3-day8 PVDF film detached/dried (1 data point lost)

  Staining:
  5.  Staining images are all from XR1 wells; GCaMP6f not imaged
  6.  Single field of view per staining condition
  7.  Classical segmentation in confluent cultures has errors
  8.  Alpha-actinin staining failed (inconclusive)

  BF video:
  9.  Low sample size for video stats (n=2-6 per substrate group)
  10. No gold-substrate BF videos at day 6 (all < 2 FPS, unusable)
  11. 10-12 FPS limits temporal resolution of beating kinetics
  12. 2x spatial downsampling may reduce sensitivity

  Fluorescence:
  13. Only 4/8 FL videos have usable FPS (>= 5); 4 are low-FPS
  14. All FL data from GCaMP6f cells only; no XR1 FL available
  15. GCaMP6f transgene may perturb CaV1 gating (Yang 2018),
      meaning the indicator itself alters the calcium dynamics
      it is meant to measure ('observer effect')
  16. Whole-field averaging may underestimate spatially heterogeneous
      calcium activity (see FL3 heatmaps for spatial distribution)
  17. No matched FL recording for XR1 cells prevents direct
      comparison of calcium kinetics between cell lines

  Cross-reference:
  18. Cross-reference is condition-level, not cell-level
  19. BF and FL were not recorded simultaneously, preventing
      direct correlation of calcium transients with contraction
      in the same field of view

================================================================================
6. CONCLUSIONS
================================================================================

  1. PULSATION ON PIEZOELECTRIC SUBSTRATE AMPLIFIES CONTRACTION
     The cleanest piezoelectric comparison (B2: gold-poled PVDF,
     pulsed vs unpulsed, same substrate) shows 2.7x higher
     contraction amplitude with pulsation. Staining on the same
     wells shows significantly different actin coherency (p<0.001),
     consistent with pulsation-driven cytoskeletal remodeling.
     Fully separating piezoelectric charge from mechanical
     pulsation alone requires Experiment 2 (nonpoled-unpulsed
     control now included).

  2. SUBSTRATE ADHESION IS CRITICAL FOR FUNCTION
     Gold-PVDF: poor adhesion (rounder cells + floating debris)
     -> reduced beating in this dataset. Non-poled PVDF showed
     the strongest contraction. Surface functionalization would
     likely improve gold performance.

  3. CELL LINE DETERMINES BEATING CAPACITY
     XR1: 11/16 active beating. GCaMP6f: 0/7 active synchronized beating
     (1/7 GCaMP6f videos showed individual cells beating).
     GCaMP6f's lack of synchronized beating is consistent with,
     but not specific for, constitutive GCaMP6f perturbing
     L-type Ca2+ channel (CaV1) gating via its calmodulin moiety
     (Yang et al. Nat Commun 2018). This mechanism was not
     directly tested here.

  4. ELECTROMECHANICAL DISSOCIATION IN GCaMP6f CELLS
     FL reveals 7/8 GCaMP6f videos have Ca2+
     transients despite 0/7 showing synchronized BF contraction
     (1/7 showed individual cell beating). This apparent
     electromechanical dissociation indicates functional calcium
     handling without coordinated mechanical output, consistent
     with partial CaV1 perturbation, immature ECC, or both.
     GCaMP6f cells are therefore not electrically silent,
     but remain mechanically uncoupled under these readouts.

  5. STRUCTURE, CALCIUM, AND CONTRACTION ARE CONSISTENT WITH A
     MATURATION HIERARCHY
     Across modalities, the data are consistent with a sequence
     from structural organization to calcium cycling to
     synchronized contraction. These stages were not all measured
     in the same cells, so this remains a cross-modal
     interpretation rather than a direct lineage sequence.

  6. NON-CONTRACTILE MOTION IS A DISTINCT PHENOTYPE
     6 videos showed periodic passive displacement without
     sarcomeric contraction, requiring expert validation to
     distinguish from true beating.
